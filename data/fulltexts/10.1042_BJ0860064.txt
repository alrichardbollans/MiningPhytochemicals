by a mechanism different from that of colchicine.

hepatectomized rats and of normal and partially

2. Although. the treatment with drugs had
little effect on the majority of the nucleotides,
colchicine caused the concentrations of NAD and
AMP to increase and those of ATP, GTP and UTP
to decrease.

Previous papers from these Laboratories dis-
cussed the determination of inositol in germinating
oats (Darbre & Norris, 1956) and in the seed and
young plants of the French bean (Darbre & Norris,
1957). Studies on inositol in germinating seeds
have been described by Burkholder & McVeigh
(1945a, b), Cheldelin & Lane (1943), Hinton (1942,
1947), Hinton, Peers & Shaw (1953) and Richardson
& Axelrod (1957); but to date there is little informa-
tion on the distribution of inositol in the dissected
anatomical parts of the seed and young plant.
The present work is an extension of that de-
scribed by Darbre & Norris (1957) and is a compre-
hensive survey of the distribution of total and free
inositol in six anatomical parts of the germinating
seed and seedlings of Phaseolu8 vulgaris at six
morphologically distinct stages of growth taking
place in total darkness.

Germination. The germination and early development of
the bean seedling was divided into six stages: (1) Dormant
ungerminated seed. (2) Steeped turgid seed (1-2 days).
(3) Elongated radicle, 1-5 cm. (3 days). (4) Appearance of
lateral roots; radicle 7-5 cm., plumule 0-8 cm. (5 days).
(5) Extension of hypocotyl; plumule 1-2 cm., hypocotyl
6 cm. (7-8 days). (6) Exhaustion of cotyledons; hypocotyl
12 cm., epicotyl 5 cm. (12-14 days).
The seeds were rendered turgid by steeping in running
water at 12-15Â° for 24-48 hr. They were then laid out on
moist filter paper supported in vacuum desiccators and
aerated by using a small compressor. For plants required at
stage 4 and later, the seedlings were transferred to layers of
gauze pegged out on large photographic trays containing
water about 1 in. deep. Complete darkness was maintained
throughout germination.
Sampling. The seedlings were removed from the ger-
minating trays at the required stage of germination and
dissected into their various anatomical parts. The number
of each part taken, usually in excess of 100, provided a
representative sample from which the mean weight per
part could be determined. The samples were weighed,
quickly freeze-dried, ground in a mortar and pestle and
reweighed. The dry powders were stored in sealed bottles
until required for moisture and inositol assay.
Method8
Moisture. After many trials with different weights,
temperatures and times, the following procedure was found
to give satisfactory and reproducible results: 0-25 g. of
sample, in a small glass dish, was heated in an electric oven
at 1000 for 6 hr.
Inositol. Total and free inositol were determined by
microbiological assay, Schizosaccharomyces pombe being
used as test organism (Norris & Darbre, 1956; Darbre &
Norris, 1957). Total inositol was measured after hydrolysis
of the sample with N-HCI at 1230 for 48 hr.
All contents are reported as ,ug. of inositol/g. dry wt. or
as p&g. of inositol/mean wt. of dissected part.

DISCUSSION
The total inositol content of the whole bean
shows a marked fall during the first 2 weeks of
growth. This phenomenon has been observed during
the germination of cabbage, maize, mung bean,
pea, soya bean and wheat when grown in the light,
and in etiolated pea and mung-bean seedlings
(Richardson & Axelrod, 1957) and in oats (Darbre
& Norris, 1956), Several authors have described
changes in inositol content during germination and
early development of seedlings (Burkholder, 1943;
Cheldelin & Lane, 1943); but valid and convincing
results had to await the development of satis-
factory methods for the extraction and assay of
inositol.

porated into the cycles of carbohydrate metabolism

ampous, Bumille & Graham, 1958), and for
Schwanniomyces occidentalis (Sivan & Hoffmann-
Ostenhof, 1961), but little is known of its fate
within the plant. Although Fernandez, Izquierdo
& Martinez (1944) reported the presence in

suggested that phytin is a storage form for carbo-

inositol could be easily converted into glucose.
A metabolic link between inositol and the sugars
and sugar acids associated with pectin and hemi-
celluloses is indicated by the production from
labelled inositol in strawberry fruit and parsley
leaves of D-galacturonosyl units ofpectin, and other
products such as D-xylose and the D-arabinosyl
and D-xylosyl residues of hemicelluloses. It is
suggested that inositol is converted into D-
glucuronic acid in plant tissues (Loewus, Kelly &
Neufeld, 1962).
The relatively small amount of phytin inositol in
the bean compared with total reserve carbohydrates
(Greenshields, 1961) suggests that the metabolic
removal of inositol during the germination and
early development of the plant under etiolated
conditions is a function of cellular economy rather
than cellular necessity.
The leaves and roots show an increase in their
respective total inositol contents, but whereas in
the leaves this rise is due to an influx of free ino-
sitol, that in the roots is due to bound forms. This
may reflect the functional differences, in the
physiological sense, of the two structures. It is well
known that inositol hexaphosphate appears to
have a particular affinity for Ca2+, Mg2+ and K+
ions in the phytin complex. The root system is
involved in the ion uptake of the plant, and the
presence of relatively large amounts of inositol
phosphates in the roots may be exerting an in-
fluence on the ionic balance.

SUMMWARY
1. The inositol content of each anatomical part
of the bean seedling has been determined, by
microbiological assay, at each of six stages during
the early development of the plant.
2. The total inositol content of the bean seedling
decreases by 40% during the first 2 weeks ofgrowth
in the dark.
3. Inositol is liberated from its bound forms
(phytin) in the cotyledons and translocated to the
growing parts of the plant.
4. The inositol content of the plumules increases
20-fold during early development of the plant, the
greater part ofthe increase being due to free inositol.
5. The increase in content of the roots (about
eightfold) is due mainly to bound forms of inositol.
6. The testas do not appear to play any part in
the metabolism of inositol during germination.
7. The hypocotyls show an increase in total
inositol content, mainly due to an influx of free
inositol. The epicotyls behave similarly.
