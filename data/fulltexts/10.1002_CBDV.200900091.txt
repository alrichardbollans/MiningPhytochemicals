A total of 186 compounds were identified in the different species and subspecies, accounting for86.9–92.8% of the total oils. The major fraction of the oil was always represented by sesquiterpenehydrocarbons (30.3–77.2%), while quantitative differences occurred between the other classes ofvolatiles depending on the taxa considered. Chemical composition of the nine Hypericum entities withrespect to the taxonomical classification was discussed. Essential oils obtained from six taxa, i.e., H.perforatum subsp. perforatum, H. perforatum subsp. veronense, H. calycinum, H. richeri subsp. richeri, H.hirsutum and H. tetrapterum, were also tested for their antimicrobial properties against five differentmicrobial strains by the broth-microdilution method, and they were found to have significant activity(expressed as MIC) on B. subtilis, moderate activity on C. albicans and S. aureus, and weak activity on E.coli and E. faecalis, the most active being those from H. hirsutum, H. richeri subsp. richeri, and H.tetrapterum.Introduction. – The genus Hypericum, belonging to the family of Guttiferae (syn.Hypericaceae), comprises ca. 400 perennial herbs or shrubs growing widely in Europe,West Asia, and America [1]; some of them were used as traditional medicinal plants invarious parts of the world [2].Italian vascular Flora comprises 30 species of this genus [3], of which ten growing inthe Appennino Umbro-Marchigiano (central Italy): the very common H. perforatum L.s.l. and H. montanum L., the common H. hirsutum L., the less common Hypericumandrosaemum L. and H. tetrapterum Fr., the rare and alien H. calycinum L., the rareand doubtfully endemic H. hircinum L., the very sporadic H. richeri Vill., H.hyssopifolium L., and H. humifusum L. The antimicrobial activities of six Hypericum essentialoils, given as minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC), are listed in Table 5. To the bestof our knowledge, this is the first report on the antimicrobial activity of the essential oilsof H. richeri subsp. richeri and H. tetrapterum. According to the statistical analysis, theGram-positive B. subtilis was the most sensitive bacteria, since it was inhibited by alltested oils (MIC values in the range of 78 – 155 mg/ml). The yeast C. albicans showed amoderate sensitivity to the essential oils (MIC values in the range of 155– 625 mg/ml),while the Gram-positive S. aureus was strongly inhibited only by H. hirsutum, H. richerisubsp. richeri, and H. tetrapterum, showing MIC values in the range of 39– 155 mg/ml.No remarkable activity was detected against the Gram-positive E. faecalis and theGram-negative E. coli. Their inhibition concentrations(<100 mg/ml) indicate a good potency level as antibiotics [17], and support their use inthe traditional medicine against infectious diseases. In the case of H. hirsutum and H.tetrapterum, antimicrobial activity of the oils cannot be attributed to any of the maincomponents (alkanes), because of their limited hydrogen capacity and water solubility[18]. The identification of volatile componentswas based on computer matching with the WILEY275, NIST05, and ADAMS libraries. In addition, ahome-made library, based on the analyses of reference oils and commercially available standards, wasused as well. The coherence of the RI values of the analyzed compounds with those reported by Adams[10] and NIST05 library [11] constituted an additional criterion in the confirmation of each compound.Whenever possible, components were identified by comparing the retention times (tR) of thechromatographic peaks with those of authentic compounds (available in the authors laboratory) rununder the same conditions. The relative amounts of essential-oil components, expressed as percentages,were obtained by FID peak-area normalization by calculating the response factor (RF) of the FID foreight different classes of volatiles (Table 2). Standard compounds, each representing the chemical classesdetermined, were selected among those available in the authors laboratory. For a high level of reliability,whenever possible, more than one standard for each class was considered. Five replicates of mixtures withequal amounts of internal standards (octane and octadecane) and representative compounds wereprepared; their final concentrations were in the range of 0.35–0.40 mg/ml, taking into account the puritydetermined for each of them with GC/FID. A correction factor of 1 was assumed for compounds that didnot belong to any of these classes. Reported response factors (RFs) were means of the RFs (average offive runs) produced by each standard within a chemical class. The following Eqn. 1 was used:RF¼Canalyte/[(Aanalyte/Ais)] Cis (1)where Canalyte is the concentration of the standard representing a chemical group (e.g., b-pinene formonoterpene hydrocarbons), Aanalyte its absolute peak area, Ais is the average of the absolute peak areasof octane and octadecane, and Cis their concentrations. Using the generalized RF for compounds withinthe eight classes, the derived quantitative data may be considered as an approximation of the absolutequantification.Antimicrobial Assay.
